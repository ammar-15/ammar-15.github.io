# Instagram: The Platform, The Reps, and the Beautiful Chaos

If there’s one platform I understand as much as YouTube, it’s **Instagram**.

Posting reels consistently, talking-head reels, carousels, educational content, selling a product, trends, memes, your niche, storytelling, etc. is what everyone will talk about. And yes, those are true, but only to some extent.

## Reps Are the Game

I do believe putting in the reps matters when it comes to getting somewhere in life. The same applies to content creation.

With each rep, you evolve. Try to do **1% better** than your previous output. Now, some reels may not perform as well as their predecessor, but that isn’t a roadblock or a sign of failure. It’s merely a hint to change the way the viewer will perceive it.

It’s all about grabbing attention, but in a nicer way.

There are plenty of formats you can find online that you can adopt to your liking. It gets overwhelming at first because everyone has their own takes. But all of the “opinions” you see online are what worked for them, or for someone around them.

So how do you tackle this?

How do you start creating content and get tonnes of views?  
How do you know what formats work for you?  
What topics can you explore?  
How do you find your niche?

## Steal the Format, Not the Soul

When you see plenty of opinions and formats online, just have them written down somewhere.

Follow the format you like for the topics you like. You might see a popular creator posting a certain reel in a certain format and realize your video topic is similar.

**STEAL IT!**

Steal the format and spice it up.

Never, **ever** copy and paste it. That’s a road that should not be taken. Unless it’s a trend, it’s still better to spice it up with your own idea to make it feel more authentic to you and your niche.

## The Niche Is You

Now, coming to the topic of niche.

Everyone will tell you to find your niche and your audience. But I’ve also seen that sometimes, **the niche is you**. And that’s the best way to go about content creation.

If you’re locked into a specific niche, the moment you try to pivot or evolve in a certain way, you risk losing your audience. In an age of short attention spans, it’s really difficult to pivot. People lose interest far too easily.

Make a separate account for pivots, and try to make **yourself** the niche.

The only one account creator I can think of who has turned their content type around is **PewDiePie**. He is the prime example of doing growing his content type as his audience grew with time.

## The Pivot Graveyard

An example of a pivot gone wrong is [**Zack Chug**](https://www.instagram.com/zack.chug/).

I started following him for his high-protein simple meal preps and fitness content. But as he grew his content into more superhero-style content, I just didn’t find it that interesting. My main motive was to save his recipes and apply them in real life. As he stopped doing that, my interest dipped.

I don’t hate his new content. But he has lost a lot of the audience that mainly enjoyed his meal prep and fitness content.

[**The Golden Balance**](https://www.instagram.com/thegoldenbalance/) is a good example of not going for a hard pivot, but slowly evolving his channel and eventually opening a restaurant.

Another example I’d like to use for a pivot gone right is [**Noel Deyzel**](https://www.instagram.com/noeldeyzel_bodybuilder/).

His main content involved fitness, motivation, and sometimes meal preps. Not really one narrow niche, but more of a broad approach. This made **him** the focus of his channel, not just his content.

When he decided to start his own [fragrance account](https://www.instagram.com/noeldeyzel_fragrance/#) because he was into fragrances as well, his new account also gained a lot of views and following.

Now, you could argue that he has good knowledge about fragrances, and since he’s providing good information about something, it will gain viewers. But a lot of his audience is also from his main account.

Why?

Because **he is the niche**.

His style of creating content gathered his main account audience, and then brought in more people from fragrance-gram back to his main account.

That is a very clean loop.

## Start With Your Actual Life

When it comes to what type of content you can create, or what topics you can cover, always start from your day-to-day life.

Anything works:

- a psychological concept
- a question that bothers you
- a product that interests you
- something funny you noticed
- something you’re learning
- something you’re tired of seeing online

Craft a story you can convey using a good hook and a format you can adopt from other creators. Just make sure you add your own twist to it so it feels unique.

This is also beautifully written in the book [*Steal Like an Artist*](https://austinkleon.com/steal/). I would recommend giving it a read.

Some of my favorite creators in the space are: [**@npcfaizan**](https://www.instagram.com/npcfaizan/) [**@brandonbalfourr**](https://www.instagram.com/brandonbalfourr/), [**@lenxpedia**](https://www.instagram.com/lenxpedia/), [**@awasaeome**](https://www.instagram.com/awasaeome/), and [**@rizwan.xy**](https://www.instagram.com/rizwan.xy/).

All of them have a unique way of telling a story that feels specific to them or whatever brand they collaborate with or topic they cover. It’s inspiring. They are the niche.

## The First Few Videos Are Basically Lab Rats

All of this may seem overwhelming, but don’t give up.

It only gets better.

Start making content. Just do it. You can worry about what works and what doesn’t later.

I heard someone say on Instagram that your first few videos will literally just be tests to see what works for you and what doesn’t. And it’s true. I agree.

A video you put **110% effort** into may not bear the sweetest fruit (views) as you would have hoped, and that’s fine. Do not be discouraged.

Try to analyze what could be done better. Try to find similar videos that worked. Re-edit the video and reupload it in two weeks or so.

**Trial and error is the only way you learn better.**

If you have access to Trial Reels, definitely give them a shot. I don’t have access to them yet, but from everyone I’ve heard who has used them, they do help.

Coming to carousels, reels, and finding your niche: **you be the niche**.

Sure, if you want to make carousels, go ahead. But do not restrict yourself to a specific format, style of content, or niche topic.

Your audience attraction funnel should have a broad and simple entry point, and then you get to a specific point or problem in your video.

This means having a simpler hook for broad attraction, then taking the viewer on a journey once they board your train.

## Production Time Will Eat You Alive

One other thing I came across is cutting down production time.

If it takes you a lot of time to create a video, you will *lose*. I realized this a month into my journey.

Have assets ready. Have them properly labelled in folders. Have your footage organized well. Cut down recording time because it really adds up to the total time.

I personally use **DaVinci Resolve**, so I can prompt Claude to make LUA scripts for automating stuff that’s repetitive.

But I would also recommend using the **Instagram Edits** app if your video doesn’t require too much editing. It’s pretty good and easy to learn.

Of course, when it comes to uploading, you need to import it first into the Edits app, choose whatever export setting makes sense for what you’ve made, and then export to Instagram with a good caption.

Make sure the caption explains or summarizes the video, and maybe even adds some extra information.

The reason I say this is because Instagram’s search feature has gotten way better, almost as good as TikTok’s. Those keywords will help your video become more noticeable.

And hashtags don’t mean everything anymore, so just add three for the sake of it and hit upload.
